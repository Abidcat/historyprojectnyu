Comparison: Jews and Muslims in Early Modern Europe

Shared Experiences of Persecution and Expulsion

In the late 15th and early 16th centuries, both Jews and Muslims in Spain faced systematic persecution and forced conversions under the rule of Catholic monarchs. The Edict of Expulsion (1492) targeted the Jewish population, demanding conversion to Christianity or exile. Similarly, by 1609, Spain's Moriscos(Descendants of forcibly converted Muslims) were expelled due to suspicions that they were secretly practicing Islam.

From a broader perspective, as noted in the " Interethnic Relations in Early Modern History", early modern Europe was marked by deep ethnic and religious diversity. Yet, this diversity was increasingly perceived as a threat to the cohesion of Christian nation-states. Both Jews and Muslims were regarded as religious and cultural outsiders, and their presence was often viewed as destabilizing.

Parallels in Forced Conversion and Secrecy

The experience of both Jews and Muslims was defined by forced conversions and subsequent clandestine religious practices. For example, Muslims forced to convert to Christianity continued to practice their faith in secret, guided by documents like the **Oran Fatwa of 1504**, which advised them on how to outwardly comply with Christian rituals while maintaining their Islamic beliefs in private.

"If they oblige you to drink wine, you may do so, but let it not be your intention to make use of it." (*Oran Fatwa of 1504*)

Similarly, Jewish conversos (Jews who had converted to Christianity) were often suspected of secretly practicing Judaism. The Edict of Expulsion specifically noted concerns about converted Jews influencing others to return to Jewish practices, stating that Christians who interacted with Jews risked being "subverted...to their own wicked belief and conviction" (*Edict of Expulsion, 1492*).

"Great injury has resulted and still results, since the Christians...draw them to themselves and subvert them to their own wicked belief..." (*Edict of Expulsion, 1492*)

Differences in Responses to Expulsion and Migration

While both communities faced expulsion, their responses varied. Many Jews fled to North Africa, the Ottoman Empire, and parts of Italy, while Moriscos primarily migrated to North Africa. The experiences of exile were also shaped by the conditions in their host countries. As described in Lives Uncovered, Jews often faced harsh conditions during their exile, with many encountering hostility or exploitation in their new homes.

Despite these challenges, the Jewish community established vibrant diasporic communities, particularly in the Ottoman Empire. Sources in "Interethnic Relations in Early Modern History" highlights that Jews played a significant role in economic networks across Europe and the Mediterranean, helping them to navigate the challenges of exile and build new lives in other regions.

What does this tells us?

The shared experiences of Jews and Muslims in early modern Spain reveal the far-reaching impact of religious intolerance and the pursuit of state-enforced uniformity. Both groups, despite different religious backgrounds, were similarly targeted as outsiders and forced to navigate hostile environments through secrecy, exile, and adaptation. However, there were also moments of collaboration and mutual support between Jews and Muslims.

This historical relationship became especially important during times of persecution. After the fall of Granada in 1492 and the expulsion of Jews, many Jewish families found refuge in Muslim-majority regions, such as the Ottoman Empire and North Africa, where Muslims offered them safe haven and a place to practice their faith freely. This act of solidarity underscores the deeper ties between the two communities, who both suffered under Christian rule but shared a legacy of cooperation and mutual respect.

The fact that these communities continued to preserve their identities under such harsh conditions demonstrates the resilience of religious minorities. The relationship between Jews and Muslims, one built on shared survival and support, highlights a broader theme of resistance against forced conversion and persecution. This comparison not only emphasizes their resilience but also offers insights into how these historical events shaped cultural and religious dynamics across Europe, reminding us of the importance of cooperation in the face of intolerance.
